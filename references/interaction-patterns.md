# Interaction Patterns

Use this file when the event needs stronger differentiation, stronger participation, or stronger memory.

## Principles

- A format is not a gimmick. It should solve a participant problem.
- Good pattern choices reduce distance, increase honesty, or increase useful connection.
- A single strong moment is often more valuable than many average segments.

## Pattern 1: Name Wall Check-In (人名墙签到)

Use when the event is large enough that participants want to know who else is present.

- What it does:
  - speeds up check-in,
  - turns arrival into a recognition moment,
  - helps participants discover people they want to meet.
- Why it works:
  - it respects each attendee as an expected guest,
  - it lowers the barrier to connection,
  - it creates a natural photo point.
- Watch-outs:
  - privacy requests must be honored,
  - numbering and pickup logic must be obvious,
  - staffing must be fast and welcoming.

## Pattern 2: No-Screen Stage (无大屏舞台)

Use when the goal is candid judgment rather than slide-heavy explanation.

- What it does:
  - pulls attention back to people instead of decks,
  - reduces performance mode,
  - makes the room feel less formal and more conversational.
- Best for:
  - founder or leader conversations,
  - peer exchange,
  - issues where nuance matters more than data density.
- Watch-outs:
  - not ideal for sessions that require visual walkthroughs,
  - moderator quality matters more because slides are not carrying the structure.

## Pattern 3: No-Master-of-Ceremony Main Stage (无总主持串场)

Use when the event already has clear session hosts and you want higher information density.

- What it does:
  - removes filler transitions,
  - shortens ceremonial time,
  - makes the event feel more intentional.
- Watch-outs:
  - transitions must be tightly scripted,
  - opening expectations must still be clear,
  - timing discipline becomes more important.

## Pattern 4: Short Talk Plus Dense Q&A

Use when a topic is valuable but audience attention is limited.

- Better than a long lecture when:
  - participants already know the basics,
  - the value lies in application or judgment,
  - the session needs pace.
- Design note:
  - keep the talk sharply framed,
  - use a moderator to surface what the audience actually wants clarified.

## Pattern 5: Two-Person Dialogue or Live Podcast (双人对谈/现场播客)

Use when the material risks becoming abstract or self-important.

- Why it works:
  - one speaker can act as the audience proxy,
  - it keeps the discussion grounded,
  - it lowers the pressure on the main guest.
- Best for:
  - practical lessons,
  - controversial shifts,
  - founder, GC, or managing partner conversations.

## Pattern 6: Sequential Challenge Panel

Use when ordinary roundtables are too polite or fragmented.

- Structure:
  - one guest speaks,
  - the next guest must respond, question, or challenge a point,
  - the sequence creates momentum and contrast.
- Why it works:
  - prevents isolated mini-speeches,
  - creates real tension and attention,
  - surfaces judgment rather than rehearsed slogans.
- Watch-outs:
  - the moderator must keep disagreement constructive,
  - guests need the right psychological safety.

## Pattern 7: Fishbowl or Open Seat (鱼缸讨论/开放空位)

Use when the audience has expertise worth surfacing.

- What it does:
  - blurs the stage-audience divide,
  - increases ownership and participation,
  - lets the room produce better examples than a closed panel might.
- Watch-outs:
  - prompt quality is critical,
  - rules for entering and exiting the discussion must be simple.

## Pattern 8: World Cafe or Structured Rotation (世界咖啡/轮转讨论)

Use when the goal is networking plus thought exchange, especially for mixed groups.

- Why it works:
  - creates multiple low-pressure conversations,
  - helps quieter participants engage,
  - increases the number of useful connections quickly.
- Watch-outs:
  - questions must be sharp,
  - timekeeping must be visible,
  - harvesting insights at the end prevents the session from feeling disposable.

## Pattern 9: Visible Participant Contribution

Use when you want emotional residue or propagation value.

Examples:

- paper-plane wishes,
- written prompts,
- public commitment cards,
- shared wall responses.

Good pattern criteria:

- aligned with the event theme,
- easy to do without explanation overload,
- visually or emotionally worth capturing.

## Pattern Choice Checklist

Pick or recommend a pattern only if you can answer yes to these:

- Does it solve a real participant need?
- Does it fit the topic better than a standard lecture?
- Can the team execute it cleanly?
- Will it improve memory, honesty, or connection?
