# AI, Growth, and Operations

Use this file when the task moves beyond concept design into operating system design.

## 1. Build a Mother Document First

The manuscript recommends a central operating document before polished outward-facing materials.

Minimum modules:

- event basics,
- timeline and milestones,
- guests and registration,
- content and agenda,
- propagation tasks,
- design and materials,
- staffing and owners,
- venue and facilities,
- budget and review.

Track each item with owner, due date, status, link, and risk note.

## 2. Use AI to Reduce Repeat Work, Not Judgment

Good uses:

- draft task lists from an event brief,
- turn a rough brief into a first outline,
- generate title options and messaging variants,
- cluster survey feedback,
- summarize notes into review structure,
- convert live notes into quote candidates or recap structure.

Poor uses:

- deciding the event's core judgment without human input,
- publishing first-draft copy without factual correction,
- replacing taste, context judgment, or guest sensitivity.

## 3. AI Prompt Chain for Event Work

Suggested sequence:

1. Clarify the brief.
2. Generate the event skeleton.
3. Stress-test from participant view.
4. Generate content variants.
5. Generate an execution checklist.
6. Summarize the event afterward into assets and lessons.

Useful prompt framing:

- "Act as a participant and tell me why this event is or is not worth attending."
- "Turn this brief into a milestone plan with owners, dependencies, and risks."
- "Generate three theme options with different tones: authoritative, warm, experimental."
- "Convert these notes into a recap outline plus five reusable assets."

## 4. Propagation as a Full-Cycle System (全周期传播)

Think in stages:

- Warm-up: recognition and expectation.
- Live: immediacy, atmosphere, and proof.
- Follow-through: recap, reuse, and continued conversation.

Simple high-value outputs:

- speaker quote cards,
- live photo selects,
- short recap videos,
- issue-based post summaries,
- follow-up reading or slide packs.

## 5. Measure What Leadership Can Understand

If the user asks for impact reporting, organize measurement into three buckets:

### Heat

- registrations,
- attendance,
- reach,
- reading or viewing signals,
- social retell moments.

### Relationship

- high-value guests retained,
- repeat participants,
- sponsor or partner follow-up,
- conversations that moved forward after the event.

### Assets

- reusable clips,
- refined templates,
- contact lists with notes,
- recurring format learnings,
- topic ideas for the next event.

Use a few stable numbers rather than a vanity-metric flood.

## 6. Convert Events Into Growth

The manuscript's conversion logic is practical:

- capture who matters,
- record who wants to stay connected,
- note who is worth deeper cultivation,
- carry the relationship into the next touchpoint.

Strong post-event questions:

- Who should receive a tailored follow-up?
- Who is likely to return, refer, or collaborate?
- Which conversation deserves a private next step?

## 7. Review for Compounding Return

Each event should leave the team with something that makes the next one easier.

Insist on capturing:

- one template worth reusing,
- one operating improvement,
- one content asset worth repurposing,
- one relationship list worth continued attention.

That is how events shift from one-off labor to an operating advantage.
