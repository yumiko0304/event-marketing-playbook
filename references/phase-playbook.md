# Phase Playbook

Use this file for end-to-end event work. It distills the manuscript into a practical lifecycle.

## 1. Define the Event Before You Design It

Write a one-paragraph brief before discussing guests, visuals, or venue.

- Audience: who is the event truly for?
- Business context: why does this event matter now?
- Promise: what concrete value will a participant get?
- Memory target: what should they remember about the organizer?
- Success signal: what will count as a win?

If these answers are fuzzy, the rest of the plan will drift.

## 2. Choose a Topic That Feels Worth Leaving the Office For

The manuscript repeatedly warns against broad, interchangeable themes.

Prefer topics that are:

- Specific to a real scene or decision point.
- Close to the participant's current pressure.
- Narrow enough to produce fresh examples and non-generic discussion.

Examples of stronger framing:

- Instead of "data compliance," use a context-specific angle.
- Instead of "law firm management," use a role-specific or stage-specific question.
- Instead of "AI trends," use a concrete workflow challenge or case.

## 3. Match the Format to the Promise

Do not default to speeches plus a roundtable because that is what everyone recognizes.

Use these matching rules:

- Knowledge transfer: short focused talks plus dense moderated Q&A.
- Real judgment and honest tradeoffs: dialogue, live podcast, or challenge-based conversation.
- Peer exchange: world cafe, structured small groups, rotating discussion.
- Community activation: fishbowl, open mic, participant-submitted questions, visible contribution moments.
- Trust and depth: smaller room, fewer stage barriers, tighter curation.

When recommending a format, explain why it supports the audience goal better than a standard forum template.

## 4. Build the Agenda Around Attention

An event agenda is not a pile of segments. It is an attention curve.

Structure the event with:

- Opening: immediate entry into the topic, not long ceremonial warm-up.
- Middle: the most substantive or interactive material before attention drops.
- Recovery points: breaks, participation moments, or energy resets.
- Closing: a clear emotional or actionable landing, not a vague fade-out.

Check for these common fixes:

- Replace long opening remarks with a sharper question-led opening.
- Break up long lectures with interaction.
- Move high-energy or participant-led moments away from fatigue zones.

## 5. Write a Proposal That People Can Actually Use

The manuscript treats the proposal as a working bridge between concept and reality.

Always include:

- One-line positioning.
- Goals and why now.
- Audience and participant needs.
- Event framework and highlight logic.
- Agenda and timing.
- Guest plan and confirmation status.
- Propagation and sponsor value.
- Execution plan, budget logic, and risk controls.

Adapt the same core logic for different readers:

- Leadership version: direction, value, risk, decision points.
- Partner or sponsor version: audience fit, exposure logic, return path.
- Internal execution version: owners, deadlines, dependencies, operating detail.

## 6. Treat Execution as Reputation

The manuscript emphasizes that small execution misses can read as a professionalism issue.

Minimum execution system:

- Milestone plan from kickoff to event day.
- Owner for every task.
- One master document with links, status, and deadlines.
- On-site run sheet with timing by minute where needed.
- Clear signage and arrival logic.
- Guest-facing details checked for accuracy and respect.
- Backup plans for lateness, no-shows, tech failure, and traffic jams.

The working standard is not "nothing exploded." The standard is that participants feel cared for and the event feels intentional.

## 7. Design for Memory, Not Decoration

Visual and spatial design are part of the event's argument.

Use design to set:

- Tone: serious, intimate, forward-looking, warm, experimental.
- Recognition: what makes the event immediately identifiable?
- Flow: where do people enter, pause, connect, and transition?
- Shareability: what are the natural photo or retell moments?

When briefing designers, focus on:

- What feeling the participant should have.
- Which moments need to feel strongest.
- Which elements must be clear, not only beautiful.

## 8. Design the Participant Journey in Three Stages

### Before

- Make logistics easy.
- Set expectation and curiosity.
- Help the right people decide to attend.

### During

- Reduce friction at arrival.
- Create at least one distinct memory point.
- Provide low-pressure ways to interact, not only "networking time."

### After

- Deliver photos, materials, key takeaways, or next steps quickly.
- Give people a reason to stay in touch.
- Turn highlights into content and future invitations.

## 9. Extend the Event Beyond the Room

Propagation is not a recap article at the end.

Build three stages:

- Pre-event: tease questions, speakers, tensions, or promised value.
- Live: capture quotes, visuals, and participant signals while energy is high.
- Post-event: recap, clips, key insights, follow-up resources, next-step hooks.

Create at least two elements people will want to repost or retell.

## 10. Convert Heat Into Relationships and Assets

Every event should answer:

- Which relationships matter most from this room?
- How will they be recorded and prioritized?
- What asset from this event makes the next event easier?

Useful outputs include:

- participant lists with interaction notes,
- guest relationship follow-up lists,
- content clips and quote banks,
- refined templates,
- lessons added back into the operating system.

## 11. Review the Event Like an Operator

Do not stop at "people said it was good."

Review across four layers:

- Attention: where did the room come alive or fade?
- Experience: where did participants feel friction or delight?
- Relationship: who stayed connected and who should be followed up?
- Asset creation: what was captured that can be reused?

Turn the review into a better template, not just a one-time memo.
